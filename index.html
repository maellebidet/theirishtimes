THE IRISH TIMES

Maëlle Bidet

Ensuring Fairer Access to Cinema in France’s Overseas Territories: What Can the State Do?

From September 27 to October 5, 2025, La Réunion will host the 6th edition of the women’s film festival Le Temps des Femmes. The event, which will crisscross several municipalities of the island, offers a rich and committed program. Despite a great success in previous years, in the overseas departments and territories (DOM-TOM), the film offer remains unevenly distributed...

An unequal distribution of movie theaters

Today, there are 24 cinemas in the DOM-TOM of more than 200,000 inhabitants (La Reunion, Mayotte, Guyana, Guadeloupe, French Polynesia, New Caledonia), but their distribution is far from being homogeneous. Guadeloupe alone has 6 cinemas, or 25% of the total. La Réunion and Martinique, with 4 cinemas each, represent 16.7% of the total, followed by Guyana, New Caledonia and French Polynesia, which each have 3 cinemas, or 12.5% of the total. Finally, Mayotte, with only one movie theater, is in a critical situation, representing only 4.2% of the total offer.
But beyond the raw number, the population/room ratio reveals an even more flagrant imbalance. In Guadeloupe, there are about 1 cinema for 64,000 inhabitants, a relatively favorable ratio. On the other hand, La Réunion reaches the figure of 1 cinema for 210,000 inhabitants, and Mayotte displays the worst ratio with 1 for 310,000 inhabitants. In metropolitan, there are around 1 cinema for 32,000 inhabitants.
Several factors explain this situation. The first is demographic: some territories have a high population density over a limited area, which makes setting up cinemas more cost-effective. This is the case of Guadeloupe, where the inhabitants are concentrated in a relatively small space. Conversely, La Réunion suffers from an uneven urban concentration that complicates the distribution of rooms across the territory. Saint-Denis and Saint-Pierre concentrate the offer (Cambaie Cinema Multiplex, Cinépalmes...), leaving areas of the East or Southeast under-equipped. As for Mayotte, the rapid population explosion and the general lack of infrastructure explain the deficit.
The second factor is economic. The opening and management of a cinema require heavy investments, which private operators are reluctant to commit in territories where the average purchasing power is lower and where profitability is not guaranteed. To this is added the cost of transporting films and equipment, which is particularly high in remote territories.

Finally, there are cultural and structural obstacles. In some territories, cultural practices favour open-air cultural events (summer screenings, local festivals, concerts), perceived as more convivial than a closed room. The rise of digital platforms also accentuates the phenomenon: Netflix, Amazon Prime, or Disney+ are becoming alternatives accessible from a smartphone, sometimes considered more practical than traveling. These developments, combined with the weakness of transport networks, reduce the demand for traditional rooms.

What solutions for better fairness?

Faced with the persistent imbalance between metropolitan France and overseas territories in terms of access to cinema, several avenues are emerging to bring the big screen closer to distant viewers. The creation of a specific fund dedicated to overseas cinemas would support the installation of new establishments. The territorial fund for cinematographic creation is already in place in some DOM-TOM but it is limited to the development of production related to cinema. Other aid could cover part of the construction and equipment costs, but also guarantee temporary tax exemptions for operators.
One of the most promising solutions would be the development of itinerant cinemas. Inspired by mobile media libraries, this device would allow trucks equipped with projectors and portable screens to move from village to village in order to transform a public square or a multipurpose hall into an ephemeral cinema for the duration of an evening. This regular event would offer a real cultural opening to the inhabitants of rural or remote areas.
Other initiatives could be based on partnerships between public and private actors. In Polynesia, associations already organize community screenings, often outdoors, which could take on a new dimension thanks to the logistical and financial support of the State and local investors.
Ensuring equal access to cinema in the DOM-TOM is not just about leisure. It is about social cohesion and cultural rights. Only strong state intervention, through funding or innovation can reduce these divides. When it comes to culture, territorial equality should not be optional; it should be a guarantee.
